Spatial Prediction of Landslide Susceptibility in Kodagu Using
Random Forest Models: Insights from the 2018 Landslide
Inventory
ABSTRACT
Landslides are among the most destructive natural hazards,
causing substantial socio-economic losses and fatalities,
particularly in the Kodagu region of the Southwestern Ghats
of India. Identifying landslide-prone zones is therefore crucial
for risk mitigation and sustainable land-use planning. This
study presents a machine learning based approach for landslide
susceptibility mapping (LSM) using a newly generated landslide
inventory corresponding to the 2018 landslide events in Kodagu
District. This article presents a machine learning model for
mapping landslide susceptibility using a newly generated
landslide inventory of the 2018 landslide events in Kodagu District
to evaluate the effectiveness of Random Forest (RF) techniques.
Optimisation techniques, namely grid search and random
search, were employed to enhance the effectiveness of RF models
in predicting landslide-prone areas, thereby ensuring a more
accurate and reliable landslide susceptibility map. Accordingly,
six conditioning factors namely elevation, slope, aspect, Stream
Power Index (SPI), curvature, and Topographic Wetness Index
(TWI) were incorporated in the analysis. The Results indicate
that although grid search achieves predictive performance
comparable to random search, it requires substantially higher
computational time. The RF classifier delineated 2502 km² and
1579 km² as low- and high-susceptibility zones, respectively,
whereas the RF regressor identified 876 km², 595 km², and
574 km² as very high, high, and moderate susceptibility
zones, respectively. The Variable importance analysis revealed
slope, elevation, curvature, and SPI as the dominant factors
influencing landslide occurrence. The RF regressor achieved
higher prediction accuracy (95%) than the RF classifier (85%),
owing to its flexibility allows it to detect gradual environmental
changes and include borderline regions often excluded by the
classifier. Therefore, the Landslide Susceptibility Map (LSM)
generated by the RF regression model is more reliable and has
more discrimination power compared to the RF classifier model.
Based on the newly generated landslide inventory map of the
2018 landslide in Kodagu, this preliminary study aims to assist
new researchers in identifying the most suitable choice between
the RF regressor and classifier for prediction modelling.
